04
Product Decisions
Problem, options, trade-offs, and what happened next

These are the decisions that changed the shape of the product. Some were strategic, some operational, some technical, and several were shared with other leaders. I have kept that distinction visible rather than rewriting the story to make every decision look solitary in hindsight.

Decision 01
Stabilisation First
Fix what's broken before building what's new
Problem
Staff were using a broken system every day while a new platform was still being procured.
Constraint
A new system would not earn trust while the existing one was visibly failing.
Decision
Fix the legacy LMS first — SSO, completions, pathways, and user guidance.
Outcome
A foundation of trust was restored before the new platform launched.

This was a shared decision made early with my managers. What I brought was the clarity about why it mattered and the structured workplan that gave it a delivery frame. A stable foundation is not a delay. It is a prerequisite.

Decision 02
G-Cloud Procurement
Choosing familiarity over modernity
Problem
The legacy LMS contract was expiring. A new platform had to be selected quickly and properly.
Options
Four suppliers were evaluated through G-Cloud scoring, written bids, and live demos.
Decision
Recommend the incumbent supplier because the ICT relationship and data architecture were already understood.
Outcome
Faster implementation, smoother SSO, and lower integration risk in a high-pressure window.
What we traded away
A more modern-looking platform from a strong contender that may have driven stronger initial engagement.
What we gained
Implementation speed, ICT familiarity, known data architecture, and lower enterprise risk at a hard deadline.
Decision 03
Migration Approach
Migration is not copying
Problem
300–500 courses, dirty data, fake accounts, and broken pathways raised the question of what should move and what should not.
Evidence
Migrating everything would also migrate every governance failure. Compliance history still had to be preserved.
Decision
Curate deliberately. Leave dirty data behind. Test every SCORM and AICC package. Back up all reports.
Outcome
Staff-rated ease of finding learning rose from 2/5 to 5/5 (≈100 respondents), alongside preserved historical data and no replication of legacy governance failures.

What was deliberately left behind: room accounts, outdated courses, incorrectly structured pathways, and edge cases that the legacy system had never resolved properly.

Governance by design: permissions were designed restrictively from day one. You can always open a system that starts restricted. You cannot easily repair a system that started open and accumulated years of ungoverned content.

Measured result: the previous ESR platform required staff to know ESR-specific wildcard search syntax, and direct course links frequently redirected to the ESR homepage rather than the intended course. Following migration, SSO, shareable course links, and contextual natural-language search replaced that experience. A live survey run across five appraisal and LMS training sessions rated ease of finding learning at 2/5 before and 5/5 after — see Outcomes for the full figure and its sampling caveat.

Decision 04
Pilot Design — 12 Months of Iteration
Not launching until the product had earned trust
Problem
Moving 13,000+ staff from paper to digital was too high stakes to launch without validation.
Evidence
Open pilot, monthly analysis, anonymised feedback review, and a cross-functional group surfaced what was not working.
Decision
Keep the pilot open for twelve months and iterate before go-live.
Outcome
Consensus that the process was easier and faster than paper before organisational rollout.

Discovery before build: before a single element of the appraisal was configured on the LMS, I met with the People Promise Manager who owned the previous process. She described a Word document that broke when people typed into it. That conversation shaped the design and also prompted an interim fix to reduce pain while the digital version was being built.

Responsible AI use in practice: personal identifiers were manually removed from feedback reports before Copilot was used for thematic analysis.

In a training session, a staff member stood up and told me directly he wasn't going to use the system. His resistance wasn't about the technology. It was about not feeling consulted. You cannot train your way out of a desire problem.
Decision 05
Rollout Strategy
Taking the product to the people
Problem
Digital communication alone would not drive adoption. The resistance was behavioural, not only technical.
Options
Rely on standard comms or build a multi-channel rollout including an in-person roadshow.
Decision
Use comms, intranet, SharePoint, and a two-week physical roadshow with clear signposting to the product.
Outcome
95–98% appraisal usage uplift in roadshow week, measured through system data.

The rollout was a team effort with named owners across the L&D team. The roadshow was planned collaboratively, and colleagues brought ideas that made the experience feel human rather than corporate.

The Director of Pharmacy pushed back because her team was already carrying too much change. What moved her was not a feature list. It was peer evidence from colleagues who said the new process was easier and faster than paper.

Decision 06
Permission Architecture
Problem
The inherited systems had too many people creating content with no shared standards.
Options
Start open and tighten later, or start restricted and open deliberately over time.
Decision
Design access restrictively from day one with gradual expansion.
Outcome
Governance failures from the legacy estate were not reproduced on the new platform.

The trade-off was slower access expansion in the short term. It was accepted because coherence was more valuable than speed at this stage.

Decision 07
Community Teams
Problem
Community nurses and AHPs operated outside the main site both physically and culturally.
Options
Run a standard central rollout or go to those teams in person with a bespoke approach.
Decision
Go to them directly — on site, in person, and with materials tailored to their context.
Outcome
Gradual but measurable growth in appraisal completions among groups a standard rollout would have missed.

The trade-off was a slower and more manual rollout. It was accepted because user segmentation only matters if it changes what you do.

Technical Decision Deep Dive
ESR assignment logic as a product decision, not just a configuration choice
Decision Log · DL-05
ESR Training Assignment — Role-Based vs Attribute-Based Design
The Problem

The LMS supplier raised a concern about using ESR Position Number as the primary mechanism for assigning mandatory training. Internal stakeholders favoured position number as the simplest and most familiar key — already used for Position Learning Needs in ESR. This created a genuine architectural conflict: two reasonable positions pulling in opposite directions, with real operational consequences either way.

The Options
Option 01 — Position Number Only
Approach
Use position number as the sole assignment key across all training types.
Benefit
Simple to configure. Familiar to internal stakeholders. Already used in ESR for Position Learning Needs.
Risk
New positions do not inherit rules. Restructures require bulk remapping. Audit trail is weak.
Option 02 — ESR Attributes Only
Approach
Use Job Role, Staff Group, and Area of Work as the primary assignment keys.
Benefit
Scales and survives reorganisation. Fewer than 20 staff groups make the rules maintainable.
Risk
Loses administrative simplicity where training need is genuinely uniform across occupants.
Option 03 — Hybrid Model
Approach
Use each mechanism where it performs best — position number for generic mandatory training, ESR attributes for specialist training, and position number as an overlay for exceptions.
Benefit
Administrative simplicity where risk is low, with better compliance accuracy where role variation matters.
Risk
More complex initial configuration and a governance requirement about which rule type applies where.
Testing the Assumption

Rather than resolve the debate in theory, a live mandatory training export was pulled to test a concrete question — do positions with multiple occupants actually have different training needs in practice? One position identified had multiple staff attached, including a secondment. On inspection, all occupants held the same role on the same ward and had no secondment-sensitive training differences in that case.

The real test is not "does one position have multiple people" — it is "do any of those people need different training." That distinction turned a binary disagreement into a scoped, evidence-based design decision.
The Decision — Hybrid Model
Generic Mandatory and Statutory Training
Primary Key
Position Number
Rationale
Training need is uniform across occupants. This is the simplest model to maintain at scale.
Outcome
Lower maintenance overhead and no bulk remapping for restructures.
Competency-Based, Specialist and Professional Registration Training
Primary Key
Job Role · Staff Group · Area of Work
Rationale
Occupants of the same position can have genuinely different needs. Attribute-based rules survive reorganisation better.
Outcome
Compliance accuracy is maintained where role variation matters.
Post-Specific Exceptions
Primary Key
Position Number as an overlay
Rationale
Adds a targeted requirement without creating a parallel rule system.
Outcome
Post-specific needs are met without undermining the scalability of the core model.
Stakeholder Communication

The same decision was communicated differently to two audiences. To the supplier, it was framed technically with direct reference to the assignment mechanism. To internal management, it was translated into plain language: what the three concerns were, why they were valid, and what we were doing about each one. One recommendation. Two calibrated explanations.

Trade-Off
What we accepted: more complexity up front and a requirement for clearer governance. What we avoided: either a maintenance problem at scale or compliance gaps in specialist training.
Success Signal
→Training assignments remain accurate for staff in complex positions, including secondments and restructures.
→No bulk remapping is required when new positions are created for generic mandatory training.
→The audit trail is meaningful — assignments can be explained by role and staff group, not only by position number.
→The supplier confirms the hybrid model is technically feasible within the current ESR integration architecture.
