What I Found
Three layers · And the people navigating them
The Technology

The legacy LMS was worse than I had been briefed. The home page was cluttered and unintuitive. Courses weren't marking as complete — in a compliance-driven NHS environment, that isn't a minor inconvenience, it's a governance failure. Staff who had changed their names through marriage found their records hadn't updated. There were fake accounts. There were accounts created for rooms rather than people. The data was dirty in ways that had accumulated over a long time with no one owning the clean-up.

ESR was its own challenge. Too many people had been given the ability to create courses with no naming conventions enforced. The result was a search experience so broken that if you wanted a user to find a specific course, you had to give them an exact string of text to paste into the search bar — including ampersand symbols before and after key terms just to surface the right result. That is not a learning system. That is a filing cabinet that nobody organised.

The Governance

The root cause of much of the technology problem was a governance one. Too many people had too much access and no shared standards. Course creation had been decentralised without any framework to ensure consistency. What felt like flexibility had become fragility — everyone could add to the system, nobody was accountable for what it looked like as a whole.

My first week I looked at the full picture and thought — can I actually do this? That's the honest answer. It was fear. Not paralysis — but the kind of fear that comes from understanding the scale of what's broken and feeling the weight of responsibility for fixing it.

Context
The inheritance · Jan 2025

This case study relates to a large acute NHS Trust — one of the UK's largest — serving nearly 20,000 staff across a complex multi-site environment. Learning and compliance aren't optional here. They are regulatory requirements with direct implications for patient safety.

When I joined in January 2025, the situation was uncomfortable from day one. The Trust had two systems running simultaneously — a legacy LMS and ESR, the NHS's national electronic staff record system. The legacy LMS had been rushed into production to support an EPR go-live. The people who configured it had already left. What they left behind was broken, poorly trusted, and generating genuine anxiety about the organisation's ability to demonstrate staff compliance.

There was no runway. No handover. No documentation that could be trusted. Just a requirements list — and a system that couldn't deliver any of it. That list became the starting point for a structured programme workplan — priorities assigned, owners named, milestones agreed. The vision was mapped before the work began.

I hadn't just inherited a technical problem. I had inherited a trust problem. And trust, once broken, doesn't respond to configuration changes.

⚠ Anxious
ICT Team
Had own pressures and systems to protect. Required patient relationship building before meaningful technical collaboration.
Director of Pharmacy
Team navigating multiple simultaneous changes. Pushed back on appraisal rollout timing. Convinced through peer evidence, not features.
✗ Friction
Information Governance
Caution by default — entirely appropriate in NHS context. Late engagement created avoidable delays. Lesson: IG should be a design input, not a sign-off gate.
Decentralised Departments
Continued creating courses on ESR without awareness of the new LMS. Not malicious — structural. Required communication, not criticism.
