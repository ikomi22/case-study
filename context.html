02
Context
The inherited environment this product had to work inside

This case study relates to a large acute NHS Trust — one of the UK's largest — serving nearly 20,000 staff across a complex multi-site environment. Learning, appraisal, and competency evidence are not optional here. They sit inside regulation, workforce assurance, and patient safety.

When I joined in January 2025, the Trust had two systems running simultaneously — a legacy LMS and ESR, the NHS's national electronic staff record system. The legacy LMS had been rushed into production to support an EPR go-live. The people who configured it had already left. What remained was a system that was broken, poorly trusted, and generating real anxiety about whether the organisation could evidence compliance properly.

There was no clean handover. There was a requirements list, a system that could not meet it, and a team at risk of looking incompetent because the platform around them was failing. That was the starting point.

I hadn't just inherited a technical problem. I had inherited a trust problem. And trust, once broken, doesn't respond to configuration changes.

02b
What I Found
Technology, governance, and enterprise constraints
Technology and Data

The legacy LMS was worse than I had been briefed. The home page was cluttered and unintuitive. Courses weren't marking as complete — in a compliance-driven NHS environment, that is a governance failure, not a small UX annoyance. Staff who had changed their names through marriage found their records had not updated. The data had become dirty in ways that had accumulated over time because no one had owned the clean-up properly.

ESR was its own challenge. Too many people had been given the ability to create courses with no naming conventions enforced. The result was a search experience so broken that staff had to paste exact strings — including ampersands — to find the right learning. That is not a discoverable learning system. It is a filing cabinet nobody organised.

Governance and Constraint

The root cause of much of the technology problem was governance. Too many people had too much access and no shared standards. What had been treated as flexibility had become fragility.

ESR also imposed a hard enterprise constraint. It is the spine of NHS workforce administration and it is not going anywhere. Salaries, annual leave, employment records, and personal data all sit there. This product had to work with ESR rather than imagine a world without it.

My first week I looked at the full picture and thought — can I actually do this? That's the honest answer. It was fear. Not paralysis — but the kind of fear that comes from understanding the scale of what's broken and feeling the weight of responsibility for fixing it.

Stakeholder Landscape
Who helped, who hesitated, and where the friction sat
✓ Supportive
Head of L&D
Understood the scale of the problem. Clear mandate provided. Key decision-making ally throughout.
Assistant Head of L&D (Line Manager)
Direct support. Flagged community teams early. Gave strategic direction on roadshow and rollout.
⚠ Anxious
ICT Team
Had their own pressures and systems to protect. Required patient relationship-building before technical collaboration became productive.
Director of Pharmacy
Her team was already navigating multiple changes. Pushback was about timing and change load, not simply resistance to the product.
✗ Friction
Information Governance
Caution by default — entirely appropriate in this setting. Late engagement created avoidable delays and became a lasting lesson.
Decentralised Departments
Continued creating courses on ESR without awareness of the new LMS. The issue was structural, not malicious. It required communication, not criticism.
