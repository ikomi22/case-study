01
Context
The inheritance · Jan 2025

This case study relates to a large acute NHS Trust — one of the UK's largest — serving nearly 20,000 staff across a complex multi-site environment. Learning and compliance aren't optional here. They are regulatory requirements with direct implications for patient safety.

When I joined in January 2025, the situation was uncomfortable from day one. The Trust had two systems running simultaneously — a legacy LMS and ESR, the NHS's national electronic staff record system. The legacy LMS had been rushed into production to support an EPR go-live. The people who configured it had already left. What they left behind was broken, poorly trusted, and generating genuine anxiety about the organisation's ability to demonstrate staff compliance.

There was no runway. No handover. No documentation that could be trusted. Just a requirements list — and a system that couldn't deliver any of it. That list became the starting point for a structured programme workplan — priorities assigned, owners named, milestones agreed. The vision was mapped before the work began.

I hadn't just inherited a technical problem. I had inherited a trust problem. And trust, once broken, doesn't respond to configuration changes.

From Vision to Structured Workplan

Before any configuration began, the programme was mapped into a structured project tracker — built collaboratively with my managers during early planning sessions. The tracker captured workstreams, key considerations, named owners, target dates, RAG status, priority levels, and approval gates. Ownership was distributed deliberately across the team and wider stakeholders — not concentrated in one person.

Prioritisation decisions were made explicitly. Recognition and the nursing framework were sequenced after the foundation workstreams — migration, mandatory training ESR link, and appraisals — because expanding the platform before stabilising it would have replicated the governance failures we were trying to move away from. That sequencing wasn't accidental. It was a deliberate product decision made at the start of the programme.

Two delivery milestones anchored the appraisal workstream from the outset: a go-live target at the start of 2026, and a new appraisal template ready by end of tax year April 2026. Those commitments were made upfront and tracked throughout — not recognised after the fact.

The Constraint You Can't Remove — ESR

ESR is a nationally mandated system that isn't going anywhere. It houses salaries, annual leave, employment records, and personal data — it is the spine of NHS workforce administration. No individual Trust, however forward-thinking, is replacing it.

The contract on the legacy LMS expired June 2025. That wasn't a soft target — it was a hard stop. Training had to move to the new LMS before the ESR API integration was complete. The decision was deliberate — waiting risked the organisation losing its primary platform with nothing functional to replace it.

For the interim period, staff were completing mandatory training on the new LMS while ESR had no automated way of knowing. A rolling banner on both systems acknowledged the delay. Structured communications managed the gap. Eventually the API was configured — but only for mandatory training. Other courses required a daily manual admin upload. Not elegant. But it worked.

04
The Decisions
Seven moments · Problem → Decision → Outcome
Decision 01
Stabilisation First
Fix what's broken before building what's new
Problem
Staff using a broken system daily while new platform is being procured
Constraint
Cannot build trust in something new while the existing thing is visibly failing
Decision
Fix legacy LMS first — SSO, completions, pathways, user guides
Outcome
Foundation of trust restored before new platform launched

This was a shared decision made in an early meeting with my managers — I want to be honest about that. What I brought to that conversation was both the clarity about why it mattered and the structured workplan that gave the decision a delivery framework. A stable foundation isn't a delay — it's a prerequisite.

Decision 02
G-Cloud Procurement
Choosing familiarity over modernity
Problem
Contract expiry on legacy LMS. Need a new platform — fast, governed, right
Process
G-Cloud framework · Scoring matrix · 4 suppliers · Written bids + live demos
Decision
Incumbent supplier recommended — existing ICT relationship, known data architecture
Outcome
Fast implementation, reduced integration risk, smooth SSO from day one
What we traded away
A more modern-looking platform from a strong contender — fresher UI, more contemporary UX that might have driven stronger initial engagement.
What we gained
Speed of implementation, existing ICT familiarity, known data architecture, reduced risk at a high-pressure moment with a hard deadline.
Decision 03
Migration Approach
Migration is not copying
Problem
300–500 courses, dirty data, fake accounts, broken pathways — what moves and what doesn't?
Constraint
Contract expiry June 2025 — migrate or lose it. Migrating everything = migrating every problem. Compliance data must be preserved.
Decision
Deliberate curation — left dirty data behind. Every SCORM/AICC tested. All reports backed up.
Outcome
Clean platform, zero legacy governance failures replicated, historical data preserved

What was deliberately left behind: Room accounts. Outdated courses. Incorrectly structured pathways rebuilt from scratch. Edge cases fixed — student nurses, maternity returners, people outside the standard data pipeline — all given documented workarounds that the legacy system never resolved.

Governance by design: The permission architecture was designed restrictively from day one — agreed with my managers as a deliberate quality decision. You can always open a system that starts restricted. You cannot easily fix a system that started open and accumulated years of ungoverned content.

Every decision was a trade-off between speed and integrity. Integrity won.
Decision 04
Pilot Design — 12 Months of Iteration
Not launching until the product had earned trust
Problem
Moving 13,000+ staff from paper to digital — too high stakes to launch unvalidated
Method
Open pilot · Monthly analysis · Copilot on anonymised feedback · Cross-functional group
Key Change
Objectives section redesigned — space to reflect on prior goals before new framework
Outcome
Staff described new process as easier and faster — consensus to launch after 12 months

Discovery before build: Before a single element of the appraisal was configured on the LMS, I met with M the People Promise Manager and the person who owned the previous appraisal process. She understood the feedback and lived experience of staff better than anyone. What she described was a Word document that had become a source of frustration — when staff typed into it, the formatting broke, fields moved, and the document became unpredictable to complete. That conversation shaped everything that followed. As an interim fix while the LMS version was being built, I patched the Word document and returned it to Mindy — reducing the immediate pain while the proper solution was in progress. That instinct to stabilise the existing process while designing the replacement is the same principle that drove the platform work from the start.

Responsible AI use in practice: personal identifiers manually removed from every feedback report before Copilot extracted thematic analysis. That's not a principle to cite. It's a practice to embed.

In a training session, a staff member stood up and told me directly he wasn't going to use the system. His resistance wasn't about the technology. It was about not feeling consulted. You cannot train your way out of a desire problem.
Decision 05
Rollout Strategy
Taking the product to the people
Problem
Digital communication alone won't drive adoption — desire problem identified
Method
Multi-channel: comms, intranet, SharePoint hub, physical roadshow over 2 weeks
Roadshow
Banners, flyers, values spinning wheel (team idea), cards to SharePoint hub
Outcome
95–98% appraisal usage uplift in roadshow week — system data, not a survey

The rollout was a team effort — coordinated across the L&D team with named owners for each channel. The roadshow was planned collaboratively, with team members empowered to bring their own ideas. The spinning wheel tied to organisational values came from a colleague — that kind of distributed creative ownership is what made the roadshow feel human rather than corporate.

The Director of Pharmacy pushed back — her team was navigating too much change at once. What moved her wasn't a feature list. It was peer evidence: colleagues across the organisation describing the new process as easier and faster than paper. Peer experience is more convincing than any pitch.

Decision 06
Permission Architecture

Access designed restrictively from day one — not to gatekeep, but because I had seen what happens when nobody does. Gradual expansion is ongoing. Governance by design is not bureaucracy. It is the difference between a platform that stays coherent and one that becomes ungovernable.

Decision 07
Community Teams

Community nurses and AHPs operate outside the main compound — physically and culturally separate. Standard rollout wouldn't land. We went to them: in-person, bespoke, on their site. Gradual but measurable increase in appraisal completions followed. User segmentation is the practical recognition that different people need different things.

What I'd Do Differently
Honest retrospective
Would Change
—Start discovery before procurement — validate requirements through user interviews, not just one stakeholder conversation
—Define explicit success metrics upfront — go-live dates existed, but adoption targets, completion rates, and data quality benchmarks should have been defined before delivery began
—Involve end users before the build — ADKAR from day one, not at launch stage
—Engage Information Governance earlier — caution that arrives late becomes obstruction
—Build more formal product delivery artefacts — user stories, acceptance criteria, explicit prioritisation frameworks. The workplan existed. The next level is product-native artefacts that scale across teams.
Would Start Earlier
—Cross-functional group — used earlier and more deliberately from the very beginning
—Structured stakeholder mapping — surface friction points before they become blockers
—User research with community teams — before designing for them, not after
—Product roadmap communication to leadership — visibility of direction reduces surprise and resistance
—Delegating earlier and more deliberately — the programme tracker named owners across the team. The next step is building that ownership culture from day one rather than growing into it.
Would Protect
—Pilot before full rollout — always advocate for this
—Governance by design — restricted access from the start was right
—Post go-live feedback mechanism — structured feedback shouldn't stop at pilot
—Going physically to community teams — meet people where they are
—Cross-functional task and finish group — just involve them earlier
07
The Five Product Lessons
What this taught me about product work

Before this role I thought product work was about managing and owning a product. I understand now it's about the relationships around it. The people who commission it, build it, govern it, use it, and resist it — those relationships determine whether the product succeeds or fails.

01
TrustBeforeTooling
You cannot build on a broken foundation. Stabilise first. Earn trust before asking people to adopt something new.
02
GovernanceBeforeScale
A system that starts restricted can be opened deliberately. A system that starts open accumulates problems that are very difficult to unpick.
03
StabiliseBeforeTransform
Discovery is not a luxury. It is the difference between solving the real problem and solving what someone thought the problem was.
04
AdoptionIsBehavioural
You cannot train your way out of a desire problem. Awareness and desire are built before the product is built — not after it is launched.
05
ProductIsRelationships
In every case — the staff member, the pharmacy director, the community teams — the answer wasn't a better product. It was a better relationship.
A developer asks

How do I build this?

A product owner asks

Should I build this — and if so, for whom, and how will I know if it worked?

"The technology was never the hard part. The human system around the technology — the trust, the habits, the competing priorities, the governance constraints — that is where the real product work happened. Build the relationships first. The product will follow."
